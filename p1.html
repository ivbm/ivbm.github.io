  • Daily Time

    • At first glance, the time visualisation would seem to suffer from the stacked bar syndrome, where it is hard to
    compare unaligned 'heights' of different segments. However, each segment can be clicked and shown in isolation,
    displaying clear knowledge of the problem as well as an interactive solution. Furthermore, the initial visualisation
    provides detailed information on mouseover. This is more readily available than the seperate graphs, and shows
    activity percentage at a given time of day.
     The ease of access is limited by the lacking height of certain segments, e.g. segments generally below 2%
    of overall actitivy. These can be difficult to pinpoint with the mouse, and as such the mouseover information
    as well as the seperate graphs are difficult to acquire. At this stage it can be hard to even identify the
    represented activity.

  • Obese Evolution

    • The interactive graph shows the evolution in obesity in the United States based on the different states, for a period
    of 14 years. The proportions of each state (normal, overweight and obese people) are shown with pie charts or target graphs.
    The diameter of the pie chart or target graph visualizes the population size, which might not be relevant for the goal
    of the visualisation of obesity in the states in USA.
    A feature of the graph is that the percentages of each category is shown in a small pop-up.
    The interactivity is very intuituve and it's a nice way of showing the evolution of obesity when moused over a
    state's pie chart or target graph.
    As each state's chart is shown over the state on the map, it is hard to compare the states and see which one
    has the most obese population, for an example.
    Without holding the cursor over a state, it is very hard to see the proportions represented in the pies.
    The states with small populations have so small charts, that is is near impossible to see the proportions.

An interactive visualization I would like to be able to do after finishing this course is, for example,
a two-dimensional scatter plot where I can see all posts from a dataset as they are mapped by emotion dimensions.

the emotions related to each post. If I would mouse over a point in the plot, I could be able to see who said what
(the user's reactions), and what values of sentiment (from -1 to 1) and arousal (from 0 to 1) were detected.
Posts should be colored taking into account the most important emotional state that characterise them.
The size of each point should show a probability score representing the importance of the classification I've done.
The chart should enable us to see interactive individual or combined emotions (for example joy and excitement both).
The intended audience of this chart would be the researcher doing the analysis or the company interested in the
posts (related to specific important events) and emotions of the users.

The interactive visualisation I would like to make is a visualisation of the interests and collection of users on the
site for game collectors called PlayRight.
On this site people who collect video games are able to document their collection. The visualization should be able to
show the proportions of video games on each hardware system, for the whole site, a specific group of people (by region
or age for an example) or a single user.
 It should also be able to show the most popular games, by year, hardware and publisher. The result could be shown by
the image of the game popping up in front of the rest of the visualisation.
 A nice feature would be if people on the site could be matched up by their interests and ratings of games.
This visualiation would help people on the site get a better overview of their collection, and maybe help them
find new games for their collection. It would also tell a story of the history of gaming, as you would be able to
see the most popular games over time.

I would like to be able to create a sort of wordnet. This would highlight a 'main' word, shown along with many other
words, perhaps ranked by the frequency of their co-occurence or something similar. From it you should be able to
navigate to different main words, either by clicking them in the presented visualisation, or by searching on them.
 Alternatively, clicking a co-occuring word could show examples of that and the main word together, either
common constructions or just simple examples. On mouseover, the words should display their co-occurence frequency.
Big frequency leaps between two words could be indicated by borders, as illustated. The frequencies would be
calculated based on a language corpus.
 The ranking of co-occurent words could be of interest to linguists interested in frame semantics or similar
topics. The exact frequency is in this case secondary to the ranking, and bar plots would confuse this relation.
